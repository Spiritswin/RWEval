Verifiers, reward modeling, and process-level supervision.  
A long line of work studies how to evaluate or supervise complex agent behavior using learned reward models, preference models, or process-level feedback rather than only final-task success. In language reasoning, verifiers have been used to improve solution selection and supervise intermediate reasoning steps, while broader reward-modeling approaches learn proxies for human preferences from comparison data (Cobbe et al., 2021; Ouyang et al., 2022; Lightman et al., 2024). In sequential decision-making and robotics, related ideas appear in imitation learning, inverse reinforcement learning, and offline learning from human demonstrations, where the central challenge is to infer whether a behavior is actually aligned with task intent rather than merely superficially plausible (Mandlekar et al., 2021; Garg et al., 2021). Our work is closest in spirit to this line, but differs in focus: instead of learning a verifier from annotated preferences or demonstrations, we study zero-shot MLLM verifiers and show that they exhibit a systematic agreement bias, i.e., a tendency to over-validate agent trajectories that appear coherent with their own prior expectations.

LLM/MLLM-as-a-judge and their biases.
Recent work has shown that strong language models can serve as scalable judges for open-ended generation and can often correlate well with human preferences (Zheng et al., 2023). At the same time, this line of work has also documented important limitations, including position bias, verbosity bias, self-enhancement bias, and reasoning failures in model-based evaluation (Zheng et al., 2023). Related concerns have appeared in studies of chain-of-thought reasoning and self-consistency, which show that models can produce plausible intermediate explanations and aggregate multiple reasoning paths, but that such explanations are not always faithful to the true basis of the model’s decision (Wei et al., 2022; Wang et al., 2022; Turpin et al., 2023). In multimodal settings, grounding remains an additional bottleneck: even highly capable MLLMs can plan effectively while still struggling to reliably connect textual intent to visual evidence on screens or webpages (Zheng et al., 2024). Our work extends this discussion from text-only judging to trajectory verification for multimodal agents, identifying a distinct failure mode in which the verifier is biased toward agreement with an apparently reasonable trajectory unless explicitly grounded in task-specific criteria.

Multimodal agents, interactive benchmarks, and self-improvement from feedback.  
The recent rise of multimodal web, computer-use, and robotics agents has created a pressing need for reliable trajectory-level evaluation. Benchmarks such as VisualWebArena for realistic visual web tasks, OSWorld for open-ended computer-use tasks, and robomimic for learning from offline robot demonstrations expose settings where final success is difficult to specify with simple heuristics and where intermediate mistakes can be subtle (Koh et al., 2024; Xie et al., 2024; Mandlekar et al., 2021). At the same time, agent frameworks such as WebVoyager and SeeAct demonstrate the growing capability of MLLM-based agents in real-world interfaces, while also highlighting persistent grounding and evaluation challenges (He et al., 2024; Zheng et al., 2024). Beyond evaluation, several works show that agents can improve from reflective or linguistic feedback, as in Reflexion and related self-improvement approaches (Shinn et al., 2023; Huang et al., 2022). Our work complements these efforts by focusing on the reliability of the feedback source itself: if verifier judgments are systematically over-optimistic, downstream self-improvement or online supervision may reinforce errors. SGV addresses this issue by eliciting explicit priors about desired behavior before evaluating a candidate trajectory, yielding more discriminative and human-aligned verification.
