Automated Scientific Discovery.
The aspiration to automate scientific research dates back to early AI systems such as BACON (Langley et al., 1987), which rediscovered physical laws from experimental data. More recently, the emergence of large language models has renewed this ambition at scale. ResearchAgent (Baek et al., 2024) demonstrated that LLMs can iteratively generate and refine research ideas over academic literature by augmenting a core paper with an academic knowledge graph and collaborative reviewing agents. Si et al.(2024) conducted a large-scale human study with over 100 NLP researchers, finding that LLM-generated ideas are judged as more novel than human expert ideas, while slightly weaker in feasibility—highlighting both the promise and limitations of current systems. Most directly related to our work is The AI Scientist (Lu et al., 2024), which presented the first comprehensive framework for fully automated end-to-end scientific discovery: generating novel ideas, coding experiments, writing full papers, and running a simulated review process, at a cost of under $15 per paper. However, The AI Scientist relies entirely on commercial closed-source APIs (GPT-4o, Claude-3.5), and its pipeline cannot be formulated as a policy optimization problem under reinforcement learning. Papers generated by The AI Scientist achieve an average simulated review score of only 4.31 with a 0% acceptance rate in our evaluation. CycleResearcher addresses these limitations by post-training open-source LLMs (12B to 123B parameters) as autonomous research agents and enabling continuous self-improvement through iterative preference optimization.
LLM-Based Peer Review.
Automating peer review has attracted growing interest as submission volumes at major venues continue to rise. The AI Scientist (Lu et al., 2024) included a review module that uses one-shot prompting, self-reflection via Reflexion (Shinn et al., 2023), and ensemble voting over multiple LLM calls to simulate reviewer opinions—without any training on real review data. Self-Refine (Madaan et al., 2023) introduced an iterative self-feedback paradigm in which an LLM generates output, critiques it, and refines it in a loop, improving performance across diverse tasks by approximately 20% on average; this self-correction paradigm informs the refinement stage of our Research-Review-Refinement cycle. For evaluation, we adopt the Proxy MSE and Proxy MAE metrics, which use the leave-one-out average of human reviewer scores as an unbiased proxy ground truth—treating each reviewer's score as an independent estimator of paper quality. CycleReviewer is trained on our Review-5k dataset, comprising 4,991 ICLR 2024 papers with over 16,000 reviewer comments, and achieves a 48.77% reduction in Proxy MSE and a 26.89% reduction in Proxy MAE compared to individual human reviewers, substantially outperforming all prompting-based baselines including The AI Scientist's review system.
Preference Optimization and Iterative Self-Improvement.
Reinforcement Learning from Human Feedback (RLHF; Ouyang et al., 2022) established the standard paradigm for aligning LLM outputs with human preferences via explicit reward modeling, but is complex and computationally expensive. Direct Preference Optimization (DPO; Rafailov et al., 2023) simplifies this by reparameterizing the reward model to enable closed-form policy optimization from preference pairs, improving stability and reducing implementation complexity. Building on DPO, SimPO (Meng et al., 2024) eliminates the reference model entirely by using the average log-probability of a sequence as the implicit reward—a length-normalized formulation that better aligns with generation objectives and reduces memory requirements. This efficiency is critical for our setting, where CycleResearcher generates academic papers averaging 28K output tokens. Iterative preference training methods (Yuan et al., 2024; Pang et al., 2024) have demonstrated that repeatedly sampling from the evolving policy to construct new preference pairs approximates online policy optimization, enabling continuous quality improvement without additional human annotation. In particular, Self-Rewarding Language Models (Yuan et al., 2024) showed that a model using iterative DPO with self-generated rewards can progressively surpass prior state-of-the-art systems, while Iterative Reasoning Preference Optimization (Pang et al., 2024) found that adding a Negative Log-Likelihood (NLL) loss term alongside DPO is crucial for stability—a finding that directly motivates our Iterative SimPO loss design. CycleResearcher is the first work to apply iterative preference optimization to long-form academic paper generation, using CycleReviewer's simulated review scores as the reward signal.
Detection of AI-Generated Content.
As LLMs grow increasingly capable of producing fluent academic text, safeguarding research integrity requires reliable detection tools. DetectGPT (Mitchell et al., 2023) introduced a zero-shot detection approach based on the observation that LLM-generated text tends to lie in regions of negative log-probability curvature of the model's probability function; detection proceeds by measuring perturbation-based curvature without requiring any classifier training. Fast-DetectGPT (Bao et al., 2023) substantially improves upon DetectGPT by replacing the perturbation step with a conditional probability curvature computation, achieving approximately 75% relative improvement in detection accuracy while accelerating the process by a factor of 340×. We integrate Fast-DetectGPT with Llama-3-8B (Dubey et al., 2024) as a mandatory safeguard in the CycleResearcher pipeline, achieving over 95% detection accuracy for review content and approximately 99% for paper text, ensuring that all AI-generated outputs can be identified and preventing misuse in real academic submission processes.
